# Writing Quality Rules

Global anti-slop rules enforced across ALL skills that produce written content.

> **Companion document:** `strategy/voice-guide.md` handles brand-specific voice, tone by context, competitor stance, and perspective (we/I/you). This document handles anti-slop enforcement, structural patterns, and citation standards. Both apply to all content. Neither supersedes the other.

## Prohibited Words and Phrases

Remove on sight. Replace with concrete, specific language.

**Hype words:**

- "cutting-edge" -- say what is actually new and why it matters
- "state-of-the-art" -- name the specific technique or capability
- "revolutionary" -- describe the actual change it causes
- "game-changer" -- state the measurable impact
- "groundbreaking" -- say what it does that did not exist before
- "next-generation" -- specify what is different from the current generation
- "innovative" -- show the innovation, do not label it
- "disruptive" -- describe the disruption with specifics
- "world-class" -- provide the evidence or drop the claim
- "best-in-class" -- compared to what? Say that instead

**Corporate filler:**

- "leverage" -- use "use"
- "utilize" -- use "use"
- "seamless" -- describe the actual user experience
- "robust" -- say what makes it durable, reliable, or complete
- "holistic" -- name the specific scope
- "synergy" -- describe the actual combined effect
- "ecosystem" -- say "system," "platform," or name the components
- "empower" -- say what the person can now do
- "streamline" -- say what step was removed or how much time was saved
- "optimize" -- say what improved and by how much

**Weak qualifiers (remove entirely, they add nothing):**

- "very"
- "really"
- "quite"
- "somewhat"
- "fairly"
- "rather"
- "pretty much"
- "basically"
- "essentially"

**AI slop vocabulary (consolidated list):**

These words appear far more frequently in AI-generated text than in human writing. One or two may be coincidence. Multiple occurrences signal AI slop.

- "delve" -- use "explore," "examine," or "look at"
- "generating" -- use "creating"
- "generate" -- use "create"
- "crucial" -- use "important" or "key"
- "pivotal" -- use "important" or "key"
- "foster" -- use "build," "create," or "encourage"
- "underscore" -- use "show" or "demonstrate"
- "showcase" -- describe what is shown
- "vibrant" -- describe specifically what makes it lively
- "tapestry" -- do not use at all
- "landscape" (abstract) -- describe the specific context
- "testament" -- state the evidence directly
- "intricate" -- describe the specific complexity
- "interplay" -- describe the specific relationship
- "enduring" -- say how long or why it lasts
- "enhance" -- say what improved and how
- "garner" -- use "get," "earn," or "receive"
- "myriad" -- use "many" or give a number
- "plethora" -- use "many" or give a number
- "nuanced" -- describe the specific nuance
- "multifaceted" -- name the specific facets
- "realm" -- use "area," "field," or be specific
- "paradigm" -- describe the specific model or approach
- "bolster" -- use "support" or "strengthen"
- "Moreover" (sentence opener) -- cut it or use "Also"
- "Furthermore" (sentence opener) -- cut it or use "Also"
- "Additionally" (sentence opener) -- cut it or start a new paragraph

**Significance puffery (remove or rewrite):**

These phrases inflate importance without adding information.

- "serves as" / "stands as" -- use "is"
- "marks a" / "represents a" -- use "is"
- "is a testament to" -- state the evidence
- "is a reminder that" -- state the point directly
- "plays a vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role" -- say what they do
- "reflects broader trends" -- name the specific trend
- "symbolizing its ongoing/enduring/lasting" -- state duration or evidence
- "setting the stage for" -- say what it enabled
- "marking/shaping the" -- say what changed
- "key turning point" -- say what turned
- "evolving landscape" -- describe what changed
- "focal point" -- say why it matters
- "indelible mark" -- say what the lasting effect is
- "deeply rooted" -- say the origin or evidence

## AI Structural Anti-Patterns

**Copula avoidance:** AI substitutes elaborate phrases for simple "is" or "has."

| AI pattern                     | Human pattern                     |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------------- |
| "serves as the primary method" | "is the primary method"           |
| "stands as a reminder"         | "is a reminder" (or cut entirely) |
| "boasts a wide range"          | "has a wide range"                |
| "features extensive"           | "has extensive"                   |
| "represents a shift"           | "is a shift"                      |

Prefer "is" and "has" over elaborate alternatives.

**Trailing -ing analysis:** AI ends sentences with participial phrases that editorialize.

| AI pattern                                           | Problem                        |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| "...highlighting its importance in the field."       | Vague claim, no evidence       |
| "...underscoring the significance of this approach." | Editorializing, not informing  |
| "...reflecting broader trends in the industry."      | Unsubstantiated generalization |
| "...contributing to the growing body of research."   | Meaningless filler             |

Never end a sentence with a participial phrase that editorializes. State facts. Cut the interpretation.

**Elegant variation:** AI avoids repeating words by cycling through synonyms.

| AI does this                                                      | Human does this                      |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| "the learner... the student... the participant... the individual" | "the learner... the learner... they" |
| "DutchwithJoost... the platform... the solution... the offering"  | "DutchwithJoost... the course... it" |

Repeat the noun. Clarity beats variation. If you called them "expats" once, call them "expats" again.

**False ranges:** AI uses "from X to Y" when there is no actual scale.

| False range                           | Problem                      |
| ------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| "from planning to execution"          | Not a scale, just two things |
| "from beginners to advanced learners" | Valid scale (proficiency)    |
| "from concept to completion"          | Sounds good, means nothing   |
| "from €100 to €500"                   | Valid scale (price)          |

Only use "from X to Y" when X and Y are endpoints on a real, identifiable scale with meaningful middle points.

**Vague attributions:** AI hedges with unnamed authorities.

| AI pattern                    | Fix                                    |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| "Experts argue that..."       | Name the expert or cut the claim       |
| "Industry reports suggest..." | Cite the specific report               |
| "Studies show..."             | Cite the study (author, year, finding) |
| "Many believe..."             | Who? How many? Or cut it.              |

Every claim needs a source. Vague attribution is no attribution.

## Punctuation Rules

**No em-dashes.** Rewrite every sentence that uses an em-dash. Options:

- Split into two sentences with a period.
- Use a colon to introduce the elaboration.
- Use a comma if the clause is short.
- Use parentheses for a true aside.

**No semicolons in marketing content.** If a sentence needs a semicolon, it is two sentences.

## Structural Patterns

**Feature to Benefit to Proof:** Every product mention must follow this pattern.

1. Feature: what it does.
2. Benefit: why that matters to the reader.
3. Proof: specific evidence (metric, case study, customer quote).

Never mention a feature without connecting it to a benefit. Never state a benefit without proof.

**Value props as undercurrent:** Thread positioning themes throughout the content naturally. Never isolate product mentions into a dedicated "Our Solution" or "Why [Product]" section. The reader should absorb value props without feeling sold to.

**Claims require proof:** Every claim must be backed by a specific proof point. Violations:

- "significant improvement" -- what percentage? What metric?
- "dramatic reduction" -- from what to what?
- "customers love it" -- which customer? What did they say?
- "saves time" -- how much time? Doing what?
- "increases efficiency" -- by what measure? Compared to what?

If you do not have a specific proof point, either find one or remove the claim.

## Customer Anonymization

When referencing customers or prospects in content:

**Keep:**

- Role title (e.g., "Head of Compliance")
- Industry (e.g., "fintech," "healthcare")
- Company size range (e.g., "Series B," "200-500 employees," "mid-market")
- Deal stage context (e.g., "during evaluation," "after 6 months of use")

**Remove:**

- Individual names (replace with role: "the compliance lead," "their CTO")
- Company names (replace with descriptors: "a mid-market fintech," "a Series B healthcare company")
- Specific identifying details that could reveal the company (unique product names, exact founding dates, specific geographic HQ unless relevant)

**Exception:** If the customer has given explicit permission for a named case study in `proof-library/case-studies/`, their name and company can be used. Reference the case study file as the permission source.

## Tone Calibration

- Write like an expert talking to a peer, not a brand talking to a prospect.
- Specificity beats abstraction. "Reduced audit prep from 3 weeks to 4 days" beats "dramatically improved efficiency."
- First person singular for founder/personal content. First person plural for company content. Never second person imperative ("You should...") in thought leadership.
- Short sentences for impact. Longer sentences for nuance. Vary the rhythm.
- Open with the insight, not the setup. Do not "throat-clear" with context paragraphs before the point.

## Citation Standards

### Source hierarchy (most to least credible)

1. **Named publications** (DutchReview, industry blogs with author attribution) — cite by name
2. **Official sources** (DUO, government sites, exam bodies) — cite by name
3. **Named expert quotes** — cite with name and credential
4. **Community discussions** (Reddit, Facebook groups, forums) — anonymize the source, keep the insight

### How to anonymize community sources

Community discussions validate reader experiences but citing "Reddit" or "a Facebook group" sounds weak.

| Instead of                          | Write                                             |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| "One Reddit user wrote..."          | "A common frustration among Dutch learners..."    |
| "In a Facebook group for expats..." | "Many expats preparing for inburgering report..." |
| "A thread on r/learndutch asked..." | "Learners often wonder..."                        |

The insight validates the reader's experience. The platform name adds nothing.

### Customer intelligence

Insights from transcripts and customer conversations inform writing but are never cited directly unless:

- The customer gave explicit permission (documented in proof-library/case-studies/)
- The quote is anonymized per the Customer Anonymization rules

## Specificity Rules

### Product implementation vs. universal requirements

Never present Dutchwithjoost's specific design as universal requirements.

| Wrong                                                     | Right                                                                                                |
| --------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Production-based learning requires 90% vocabulary usage" | "Dutchwithjoost considers a lesson complete when you've used most of the vocabulary in conversation" |
| "This is what effective learning requires"                | "This is how we designed the course"                                                                 |
| "You need 2+ successful uses per word"                    | "In our course, each word needs multiple successful uses before it counts"                           |

If describing how the product works, be explicit: "Dutchwithjoost does X" not "Learning requires X."

### Advice sections

When giving advice (e.g., "What to look for in a Dutch course"), criteria must be:

- Genuinely useful even if the reader chooses a competitor
- Applicable to multiple options, not just us
- Not suspiciously specific to our product's features

Test: If a competitor satisfied these criteria, would you be happy? If no, you've written criteria to disqualify competitors, not to help readers.

## Sales and Pricing Rules

### Price mentions

**Include pricing in:**

- BOFU content (comparison pages, pricing pages, "is X worth it" articles)
- Direct response content where price is the point
- Product pages

**Never include pricing in:**

- MOFU thought leadership content
- Educational articles where you're building trust through insight
- Sections that would otherwise feel like advice

Price mentions in thought leadership break the frame. The reader was learning, now they're being sold to.

### Sales language in educational content

Educational content should thread value props naturally, not isolate them into sales sections.

| Wrong                                       | Right                                                 |
| ------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Dedicated "Why Dutchwithjoost" section      | Value props woven into relevant context               |
| "Our solution offers..."                    | Mention the feature where it naturally fits the topic |
| Listing price in a thought leadership piece | Keep pricing for dedicated product content            |

The reader should absorb value props without feeling the shift from education to sales.
