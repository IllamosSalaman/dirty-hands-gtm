# Common Dutch Mistakes — the grounding bank for `mistake` posts

The source bank for the **`mistake`** Instagram post type. One post takes **one** confusable pair or
high-frequency error and teaches it: a cover that names the pair (`niet` vs `geen`), an intro that states
the difference in one line, a card for **each side** (the rule + two example pairs), a "quick test" card,
and the CTA. The rendered `ConceptGuide` carousel (variant: `mistake`) expects exactly this shape. This is
**teaching**, not a quiz: show how to use each one correctly and when each applies.

> **Companion files.** This bank is the *teaching* layer; `dutch-grammar.md` is the *checker*. Where they
> overlap (niet/geen, word order, register), keep them consistent: this file explains it to a viewer,
> `dutch-grammar.md` verifies the Dutch in the props. Every example line stays **A1** and carries its
> English gloss (`dutch-level-guide.md`). Not topic-bound: a `mistake` post can sit in any week.

For each entry: **the mistake** (what learners actually do), **each side** with the rule and example
pairs, and a **quick test**. Right/wrong pairs are written as `✅ correct` / `❌ wrong` so the post can
show the contrast directly.

---

## 1. `niet` vs `geen` (the flagship)

**The mistake:** using `niet` to negate a noun that needs `geen` (`Ik heb niet tijd`).

- **`geen`** negates an **indefinite noun** (a noun with `een`, or with no article at all).
  - `Ik heb geen tijd.` — I have no time.
  - `Dat is geen probleem.` — That is not a problem.
  - `Ik spreek geen Frans.` — I do not speak French.
- **`niet`** negates **everything else**: verbs, adjectives, definite nouns, the whole clause.
  - `Ik begrijp het niet.` — I do not understand it.
  - `Het is niet moeilijk.` — It is not difficult.
  - `Ik woon niet in Amsterdam.` — I do not live in Amsterdam.

**Quick test:** could you say "no / not a / not any" in English? Then it is `geen`. Otherwise `niet`.
`❌ Ik heb niet tijd.` → `✅ Ik heb geen tijd.`

## 2. `kennen` vs `weten` (both "to know")

**The mistake:** `weten` for a person or place (`Ik weet hem`).

- **`kennen`** = to know / be familiar with a **person, place, or thing** (takes a noun object).
  - `Ik ken Amsterdam.` — I know Amsterdam.
  - `Ken je hem?` — Do you know him?
- **`weten`** = to know a **fact** (often with `dat...`, `waar...`, `hoe...`, or `het`).
  - `Ik weet het niet.` — I do not know.
  - `Weet je waar hij woont?` — Do you know where he lives?

**Quick test:** a person or place → `kennen`. A fact or piece of information → `weten`.
`❌ Ik weet hem.` → `✅ Ik ken hem.`

## 3. `de` vs `het` (which article)

**The mistake:** guessing the article (`het tafel`), or dropping it from memory.

- Most nouns are **`de`** (about two thirds). All **plurals** are `de` (`de huizen`, `de kinderen`).
- **`het`** is the smaller group, and you mostly memorize it, but two patterns are reliable:
  - every **diminutive** (`-je`) is `het`: `het meisje`, `het kopje`, `het broodje`.
  - one thing, neuter, indefinite → bare adjective: `een groot huis` (not `een grote huis`).

**Quick test:** there is no full rule, so **learn the article with the noun**, never the noun alone.
`❌ het tafel` → `✅ de tafel`. (Tip for a post: teach a batch of common `het`-words people miss.)

## 4. `er` (the little word learners drop)

**The mistake:** leaving out `er`, or never knowing which `er` to use. Four jobs:

- **there is / there are:** `Er is koffie.` — There is coffee.
- **there (place):** `Ik ben er al.` — I am already there.
- **of them (with a number):** `Ik heb er twee.` — I have two (of them).
- **er + preposition (for things):** `Ik denk er niet aan.` — I am not thinking about it.

**Quick test:** if an English sentence has "there is/are", "of them", or "about it / on it", Dutch
probably needs `er`. `❌ Ik heb twee.` → `✅ Ik heb er twee.`

## 5. `als` vs `dan` (comparisons)

**The mistake:** `groter als` for "bigger than" (common in speech, wrong in standard Dutch).

- **`dan`** for an **unequal** comparison ("more / -er than").
  - `Hij is groter dan ik.` — He is taller than me.
  - `Dit is beter dan dat.` — This is better than that.
- **`als`** for an **equal** comparison ("as ... as"), and for "if / when".
  - `Zij is net zo lang als ik.` — She is as tall as me.
  - `Als het regent, blijf ik thuis.` — If it rains, I stay home.

**Quick test:** "-er than" → `dan`. "as ... as" or "if/when" → `als`.
`❌ groter als ik` → `✅ groter dan ik`.

## 6. `naar` vs `na` (one letter, very different)

**The mistake:** `na huis` for "to home" (they sound close).

- **`naar`** = **to** (direction): `Ik ga naar huis.` — I am going home.
- **`na`** = **after** (time): `Na het werk ga ik sporten.` — After work I go to the gym.

**Quick test:** movement toward a place → `naar`. A point in time you come after → `na`.
`❌ Ik ga na huis.` → `✅ Ik ga naar huis.`

## 7. `liggen` vs `leggen` (and `staan` vs `zetten`)

**The mistake:** using one verb for both "to lie" and "to lay".

- **`liggen` / `staan`** = the thing **is** somewhere (no object). `Het boek ligt op tafel.` — The book is
  (lying) on the table. `De fles staat in de kast.` — The bottle is (standing) in the cupboard.
- **`leggen` / `zetten`** = **you put** the thing there (takes an object). `Ik leg het boek op tafel.` —
  I put the book on the table. `Ik zet de fles in de kast.` — I put the bottle in the cupboard.

**Quick test:** is something just lying/standing there (`liggen`/`staan`) or are you placing it
(`leggen`/`zetten`)? `❌ Ik lig het boek op tafel.` → `✅ Ik leg het boek op tafel.`

## 8. Word order: inversion after a fronted phrase

**The mistake:** keeping the English subject-first order after starting with a time or place
(`Morgen ik werk`).

- When **anything other than the subject** comes first, the verb stays in **position two**, so the
  subject moves to **right after the verb**.
  - `❌ Morgen ik werk.` → `✅ Morgen werk ik.` — Tomorrow I work.
  - `❌ In Amsterdam hij woont.` → `✅ In Amsterdam woont hij.` — He lives in Amsterdam.

**Quick test:** did you start with something that is not the subject? Then verb second, subject third.
(See `dutch-grammar.md` §1.2.)

## 9. Separable verbs: the prefix jumps to the end

**The mistake:** keeping a separable verb joined in a main clause (`Ik opbel je`).

- In a main clause the prefix **splits off and goes to the end**.
  - `❌ Ik opbel je morgen.` → `✅ Ik bel je morgen op.` — I will call you tomorrow.
  - `❌ Ik meekom.` → `✅ Ik kom mee.` — I am coming along.
- It stays joined as an infinitive at the end: `Ik wil je morgen opbellen.`

**Quick test:** is the verb one like `opbellen`, `meekomen`, `aankomen`, `uitgaan`? In a main clause,
send the prefix to the end.

## 10. `u` vs `je` (the register mistake)

**The mistake:** mixing formal and informal in one line (`Kunt u jouw naam zeggen?`), or using `je` with
a stranger.

- **`u`** for strangers, shop and counter staff, officials. Keep `u` + `uw` together.
  - `✅ Kunt u uw naam zeggen?` — Could you say your name?
- **`je` / `jij`** for friends, peers, a study partner. Keep `je` + `jouw` together.
  - `✅ Kun je je naam zeggen?` — Can you say your name?

**Quick test:** one register per sentence. `❌ Kunt u jouw naam zeggen?` mixes `u` with `jouw`
→ `✅ Kunt u uw naam zeggen?` (See `dutch-grammar.md` §7.)

## How a `mistake` post uses this bank

- **One pair/error per post.** Cover = the pair (`niet` vs `geen`). Intro slide = the one-line
  difference. Then a card per side (rule + two examples), a "quick test" card, the CTA.
- Show the contrast with `✅` / `❌` so the wrong version is visible and corrected on the same slide.
- Keep every example **A1**, always glossed. The error being corrected can be a sentence learners really
  write; the fix must be A1-clean.
- Caption line 1 = a searchable keyword ("niet or geen", "kennen vs weten", "Dutch word order mistake").
- Pin a comment that pulls a sentence reply ("which pair still catches you out?").
