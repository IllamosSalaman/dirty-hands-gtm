# Dutch Modal Particles — the grounding bank for `particle` posts

The source bank for the **`particle`** Instagram post type. One post teaches **one** particle: a cover,
a short "what is it and how does it work" intro, then a card per **sense or use** with two real example
sentences each (the shape the rendered `ConceptGuide` carousel expects). Write a `particle` post *from*
the entry here. Do not invent uses or examples from memory: these words are subtle and a wrong nuance is
the kind of thing a native speaker spots instantly.

> **Companion files.** Correctness is still checked against `dutch-grammar.md`; level against
> `dutch-level-guide.md`. Modal particles are a **sanctioned A2 exception** (see `dutch-level-guide.md`
> → *Sanctioned exceptions*): the particle itself is the advanced thing being taught, so it may sit
> above strict A1, but the **example sentences around it stay A1**, every line carries its English
> gloss, and the explanation is plain. The overlap with `sound-more-dutch.md` is deliberate and
> different in framing: that file is the conversational *stance/filler* layer ("words that make you
> sound Dutch"); this file is the *grammar-of-tone* layer (how a particle bends a sentence's meaning).

## What a modal particle is (for the intro slide)

Dutch sprinkles tiny unstressed words into a sentence that change its **tone**, not its facts:
`even`, `maar`, `nou`, `toch`, `hoor`. They are how a sentence sounds friendly, impatient, reassuring,
or casual. English does the same job with intonation and words like "just", "though", "you know", or
"go ahead". Learners leave them out and sound flat or blunt; using one well is the fastest way to sound
like a person instead of a textbook.

The honest framing for a post: **you cannot fully translate a particle, you learn what it does.** Teach
one particle, show two or three of its everyday uses, and let the example pairs carry the feel.

## The ten core particles, grouped by tone

Per the research (L. Pronk, *Dan nou maar eens even*, neerlandistiek.nl) the ten that do most of the work
are: **dan, eens, even, maar, misschien, nou, ook, soms, toch, wel**. Grouped by what they do to the tone
(after *Zichtbaar Nederlands*):

| Group | Particles | What they do |
|---|---|---|
| **Soft / softeners** | `even`, `misschien`, `maar` | shrink a request, make it casual and low-effort, give permission |
| **Neutral** | `gewoon`, `eens`, `soms`, `ook` | downplay, nudge, soften a question, add "too / after all" |
| **Sharper / forceful** | `nou`, `toch` | urge, insist, appeal to something you both already know |
| **Affirming** | `wel`, `dan` | counter an assumed "no", answer a "then what?" |
| **Sentence-final tag** | `hoor` (+ `joh`, `zeg`) | reassure, soften, sound warm (not one of the ten, but everywhere) |

A `particle` post picks **one** of these and walks its uses. Below: the flagship (`nog`), then each
particle with its uses and example pairs.

## Flagship: `nog` (the calibration post)

`nog` is the best first `particle` post: one small word, a fan of clear senses, each with two short
examples. (Strictly `nog` is a high-frequency adverb rather than one of the ten tone-particles, but it
behaves the same way for teaching: meaning shifts with position and pairing.)

Intro line: *In Dutch, `nog` is a small word that does a lot. There is no single English match, so its
meaning depends on where it sits and what it pairs with.* In English it can land as: still, not yet,
more, again, even, only, a little longer.

| Sense | Example (NL) | EN | Tip |
|---|---|---|---|
| **still** (continues) | `Het regent nog.` | It is still raining. | use `nog` when something keeps going |
| **still** | `Ben je nog moe?` | Are you still tired? | |
| **more / another** | `Wil je nog koffie?` | Do you want more coffee? | `nog` = add more of something |
| **even + comparison** | `Zij is nog langer.` | She is even taller. | `nog` strengthens a comparison |
| **not yet** (`nog niet`) | `Ik ben nog niet klaar.` | I am not ready yet. | pair with `niet` |
| **only** (`nog maar`) | `Het is nog maar maandag.` | It is only Monday. | `nog maar` = only / just |
| **only** (`nog maar`) | `Ik heb nog maar drie euro.` | I only have three euros left. | |
| **a little longer** (`nog even`) | `Ik blijf nog even.` | I will stay a little longer. | `nog even` = a moment more |
| **left / remaining** | `Hoeveel tijd hebben we nog?` | How much time do we have left? | for time, distance, quantity |
| **left / remaining** | `Nog twee kilometer.` | Two more kilometers. | |

## `even` (softener)

Makes an action sound small, quick, and no-big-deal. Often "for a sec / just / quickly".

- `Kun je me even helpen?` — Can you help me for a sec?
- `Ik bel je even.` — I will give you a quick call.
- `Wacht even.` — Hold on a moment.
- Tip: `even` shrinks the ask, so the other person feels it costs them nothing.

## `maar` (softener / permission)

As a particle (not the word "but"), `maar` softens an imperative into a warm "go ahead".

- `Zeg het maar.` — Go ahead and say it.
- `Kom maar binnen.` — Come on in.
- `Doe maar.` — Go ahead. / That is fine.
- Also "only / just" with quantities: `Ik heb maar twee euro.` — I only have two euros.
- Tip: imperative + `maar` = encouragement, not an order.

## `misschien` (softener)

"Maybe" — turns a request into a gentle suggestion the other person can refuse.

- `Kun je misschien wat langzamer praten?` — Could you maybe speak a bit slower?
- `Zullen we misschien koffie doen?` — Shall we maybe get coffee?
- Tip: adds an easy way out, so the request feels polite.

## `gewoon` (neutral, downplayer)

"Just / simply" — removes fuss, says "no special trick here".

- `Praat gewoon Nederlands tegen me.` — Just speak Dutch to me.
- `Doe gewoon normaal.` — Just act normal.
- `Het is gewoon zo.` — That is just how it is.
- Tip: `gewoon` makes a thing sound easy and ordinary.

## `eens` (neutral, gentle nudge)

Often "een keer / sometime"; with an imperative it nudges softly. Spoken as "es" / "'ns".

- `Kom eens langs.` — Come visit sometime.
- `Luister eens.` — Listen for a second.
- `Probeer het eens.` — Give it a try.
- Tip: `eens` makes a suggestion feel light, never pushy.

## `soms` (neutral; "by any chance" in questions)

Usually "sometimes". In a question it can soften into "by any chance / happen to".

- `Ik maak soms fouten.` — I sometimes make mistakes.
- `Heb je soms een pen?` — Do you happen to have a pen?
- Tip: in a question, `soms` makes it less direct.

## `ook` (neutral; "also / after all")

Usually "also / too". As a particle it adds "after all / indeed", explaining why.

- `Ik ga ook mee.` — I am coming too.
- `Ik ben ook moe.` — I am tired, after all. (as the reason)
- Tip: end-of-sentence `ook` often justifies what you just did.

## `nou` (forceful; urging, or "well...")

Pushes or urges; also the everyday "well" that opens a thought.

- `Schiet nou op!` — Come on, hurry up!
- `Doe nou normaal.` — Come on, behave.
- `Nou, ik weet het niet.` — Well, I am not sure.
- Tip: `nou` after a verb adds impatience; at the start it is just "well".

## `toch` (forceful; "right? / anyway / after all")

The hardest one, and worth its own post. `toch` appeals to something you both already know, asks for
confirmation, or pushes back against an assumed "no".

- `Je komt toch wel?` — You are coming, right? (seeking a yes)
- `Doe toch normaal!` — Come on, behave! (mild insistence)
- `Het is toch zo?` — It is true, isn't it?
- `Ik doe het toch.` — I will do it anyway.
- Tip: `toch` leans on shared knowledge or expectation.

## `wel` (affirming; the opposite of `niet`)

Affirms, often countering an assumed negative. Also reassuring "it'll be fine".

- `Ik heb het wel gedaan.` — I did do it. (you thought I hadn't)
- `Ik wil wel.` — I would be happy to.
- `Het komt wel goed.` — It will be fine.
- Tip: `wel` answers a doubt with a quiet "yes, actually".

## `dan` (affirming; "then / in that case")

Beyond "then" in time, `dan` answers "then what?" and tidies a reply.

- `Doe het dan zelf.` — Then do it yourself.
- `Wat wil je dan?` — What do you want, then?
- Tip: `dan` reacts to what was just said.

## `hoor` (the friendly sentence-final tag)

Not one of the ten, but everywhere in warm, reassuring speech. It sits at the **end** and softens the
whole line into "really, it's fine".

- `Het is goed hoor.` — It is fine, really.
- `Dank je wel hoor.` — Thanks a lot.
- `Geen probleem hoor.` — No problem at all.
- Tip: `hoor` cannot start a sentence; it closes one warmly. (Cousins: `joh`, `zeg`.)

A `hoor` post can also pair it with `joh` and `zeg` as "the little words Dutch people end sentences with".

## How a `particle` post uses this bank

- **One particle per post.** Cover names the particle. Intro slide = "what it does + you can't translate
  it, you learn it". Then one card per sense/use with two example pairs. Close on the standard CTA.
- **Five to eight sense-cards** is the sweet spot (the `nog` table above is a ready set).
- Keep every example sentence **A1** (short, common words, present tense) even though the particle is the
  advanced payload. Always show the English gloss.
- Caption line 1 = a searchable keyword ("how to use *nog* in Dutch", "Dutch word *toch* meaning").
- Pin a comment that pulls a sentence reply ("which little word trips you up most?").
