## Passing the exam is not the same as keeping the Dutch

Passing the inburgering exam and keeping the Dutch are two different outcomes. The difference comes down to **how a course defines done**. Most courses count you finished when you complete the exercises, which trains you to recognize the right answer. A few count you finished when you can use the language yourself, which trains you to produce it.

The exam asks for both. The reading and listening parts ask you to recognize correct answers. The speaking and writing parts ask you to produce Dutch yourself, and the A2 writing exam is still done with pen and paper. So a course that mainly trains recognition can carry you through the exam and still leave you unable to say much a year later.

This pattern shows up again and again in learner interviews. One learner described finishing an earlier course this way: "I did it, I finished it. But I didn't feel like I retained anything." Another person passed through a memorization-based course and could not pronounce the word inburgering on the day he collected his passport.

Understanding Dutch and producing Dutch are different skills. Research on production versus comprehension learning found that learners who practiced producing a language outperformed those who only practiced understanding it, on both vocabulary and grammar (Hopman and MacDonald, Psychological Science, 2018). That is why the way a course defines completion matters more than its price.

Most self-paced courses are finished when you have completed the lessons. A course like DutchwithJoost is finished when you can have the conversation. For why using the language is what makes it stick, see our explainer on production-based learning. The rest of this comparison runs that one question through every option.

## First, check your track and funding route

Before comparing courses, check two things about your own situation: the level you need to reach, and how you are paying. Both narrow your options before price or method matters. Get them wrong and you can buy the right-feeling course for the wrong requirement.

**Your required level.** Under the 2013 integration law (Wet inburgering 2013), the standard requirement is A2. Under the 2021 law (Wet inburgering 2021), your municipality assigns a learning route during your intake: the B1 route (B1 level), the education route (B1 or higher), or the self-reliance route (A1, with A2 optional). Confirm which applies to you, because a course built for A2 will not get you to B1. If you are unsure which level you need, check our guide on A2 versus B1 for inburgering.

**Your funding route.** If you are paying with a DUO loan (DUO is the Dutch education agency that handles integration funding), you have to choose a school certified by Blik op Werk, the Dutch quality mark for integration courses. That narrows the list to approved providers. Self-paced courses that are not on that list can still be strong self-funded choices or supplements, so they are worth comparing if you are paying yourself. People integrating voluntarily and those pursuing citizenship can also borrow from DUO within three years of registering with their municipality. For a full breakdown of what each option costs, see our guide to inburgering course costs.

## FAQ

### Which inburgering course actually teaches you Dutch?

The courses that teach Dutch you keep are the ones where finishing a lesson means using the language, not just completing exercises. Most self-paced courses such as Inburgering.org, InburgeringOnline, and InburgeringPrep train recognition, so you can pick the right answer on the exam but need to keep practicing afterward to retain it. Classroom courses like Koentact add real speaking time. DutchwithJoost completes a lesson only once you have used the new words in conversation across all four skills, so you learn by using the language rather than memorizing it.

### Will I forget Dutch after passing the inburgering exam?

It depends on how you learned it. If you trained mainly to recognize correct answers and memorized for the exam, that knowledge fades quickly once you stop. What sticks is the language you actually produced and used. If you practiced speaking and writing across real situations, you keep far more of it. This is why the way a course defines completion matters more than its price.

### What is the cheapest inburgering course?

Inburgering.org is the lowest-priced option, at roughly €60 to €120 total to reach A2 (from about €4.99 a month, as of 2026, so verify before relying on it). It follows the official exam format closely, which makes it a solid route to passing. The honest caveat: it is memorization-based, so plan to keep practicing on your own afterward if you want to keep using Dutch.

### Are expensive inburgering courses worth it?

It depends on what the higher price buys you. Classroom courses run roughly €500 to €2,000 for A2 and give you a real teacher, structure, and genuine speaking time with other people, which helps retention. The question to ask is whether the spend buys you the ability to use Dutch afterward or just more exam practice. A more expensive course is worth it when it gets you producing the language.

### Can I use the DUO loan for any inburgering course?

No. If you pay with a DUO loan, you must choose a school certified by Blik op Werk, which narrows your options. Self-paced courses that are not on the certified-provider list can still be excellent self-funded choices or supplements, but they are not loan-eligible. Before you compare courses, confirm your required level (A2 under the 2013 law, or B1 under the 2021 law depending on your assigned route) and your funding route.
