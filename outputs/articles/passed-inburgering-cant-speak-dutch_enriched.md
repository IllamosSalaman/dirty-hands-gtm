### Section 2: Why Recognition-Based Learning Creates This Gap
**Word target:** 350
**Key argument:** Explain the cognitive science. Recognition is easier than production. Picking the right answer is not the same as generating the sentence.

**Evidence:**

1. **Foundational theory (peer-reviewed, highly citable):**
   - Swain's Output Hypothesis (1985): "Comprehensible input alone is insufficient for full language development. Production forces learners from semantic processing (understanding meaning) to syntactic processing (producing correct grammar)."
   - Based on Canadian French immersion programs where students with years of input exposure still lacked grammatical competence in production.

2. **Empirical study (peer-reviewed):**
   - Hopman & MacDonald (2018): "Production-learning participants outperformed comprehension-learning participants on both vocabulary comprehension AND grammatical comprehension tests. Production practice provides a stronger learning experience than comprehension practice alone."
   - Key insight: Production practice improves both production AND comprehension. Recognition practice only improves recognition.

3. **Testing effect research (landmark study):**
   - Karpicke & Roediger (2008): "Repeated studying after learning had no effect on delayed recall. Repeated testing produced a large positive effect on retention."
   - Students' predictions of their own performance were uncorrelated with actual results — learners underestimate retrieval benefits.

4. **Education publisher (citable):**
   - Seidlitz Education: "This transfer does not happen on its own." — Active vocabulary requires deliberate production practice.

5. **Customer interview insight:**
   - "The words that you learn you use... If I'm learning new words and I'm using them in a sentence and I know in which context I'm using them... it helps me communicate." (Nina interview — describes what production learning feels like)

### Section 3: How Memorization Courses Are Designed (And Why)
**Word target:** 300
**Key argument:** Recognition-based courses aren't malicious — they're optimized for the wrong outcome. Faster to build, easier to measure, learners feel progress.

**Evidence:**

1. **Customer interview (direct experience):**
   - On Taalthuis: "It was actually very boring. I would spend two hours a day on it... a bunch of really boring grammar rules that I had to memorize... no speaking at all actually, just memorizing words and rules... I did it, I finished it. But I didn't feel like I retained anything. I just was happy to finish it." (Nina interview)
   - On InburgeringOnline: "It's basically cards to memorize. It's memorization... It's a cheat sheet to the exam." (Nina interview)
   - On UvA course: "The UX of the platform was fucking horrible... between lesson one and lesson two, the jump of difficulty was insane. It wasn't incremental... I would translate it right but they would say it's wrong because they expect exact wording." (Nina interview)

2. **Structural explanation (from knowledge base):**
   - "Building a course around exercises is easier to measure. '80% of exercises completed' is easy to track. 'Can form sentences using the vocabulary' requires testing actual production."
   - Most courses measure progress by exercises completed: click through screens, match words to pictures, fill in blanks, pick the correct option.

3. **Competitor analysis (factual, not dismissive):**
   - Success metric in reviews: "Within 45 days I passed all exams" — passing speed, not retention.
   - Gap: They dominate "pass the exam" but don't own "actually speak Dutch afterward."

### Section 4: The Speaking Exam Doesn't Fix This
**Word target:** 250
**Key argument:** Even courses with speaking components treat it as one more thing to cram for, not as proof throughout.

**Evidence:**

1. **Named publication (citable):**
   - DutchReview: "The spreken examen is often named as one of the hardest exams for internationals because, unlike with reading, listening, or writing, there's almost no chance to edit your work."

2. **Customer interview:**
   - "A lot of people struggle with that one. That's one of the most painful exams, is the speaking. Because the course they took, they don't really teach them that." (Nina interview)
   - "If you want to learn speaking, you have to practice speaking to be able to learn speaking... if you want a teacher, that's too expensive." (Nina interview)

3. **Structural problem:**
   - Courses follow a predictable pattern: reading module, listening module, writing module, speaking module. Speaking comes last, after months of recognition training.
   - By then, learners have strong recognition skills and weak production skills. They memorize phrases for the speaking exam like they memorized vocabulary.

4. **Named publication (citable):**
   - Dutch-online.com: "Most learners don't fail because they don't know Dutch — they underestimate speaking and writing."

### Section 5: What Production-Based Learning Looks Like
**Word target:** 350
**Key argument:** The difference is what counts as "done." Production-based = lessons complete when you've used the vocabulary, not when you've finished exercises.

**Evidence:**

1. **Theoretical foundation (peer-reviewed):**
   - Bjork & Bjork (2011) "Desirable Difficulties": "Spacing, interleaving, and retrieval practice are 'desirable difficulties.' These strategies feel harder during practice but produce dramatically better long-term outcomes."
   - "Learners often underestimate the effectiveness of difficult practice."

2. **Retrieval threshold (peer-reviewed):**
   - Nakata (2017): "5+ retrievals produced significantly better retention than 1-3 retrievals." There is a threshold of retrieval practice needed for durable learning.

3. **Customer interview (what good learning feels like):**
   - "With Dutchwithjoost, it's the words that you learn you use... If I'm learning new words and I'm using them in a sentence and I know in which context I'm using them, it helps me communicate." (Nina interview)
   - "In my past courses, I would just learn words here and there. I really struggle to see how they come together... I keep memorizing shit that doesn't stick because I don't know how to use it." (Nina interview)
   - "There is no memorizing which I really like because I really hated it so much to spend two hours on one rule with memorizing a million words that don't make sense together." (Nina interview)

4. **Level-matching (addresses common problem):**
   - "Joost knows your level... he's not gonna use C2 level Dutch to reply to you." (Nina interview)
   - Contrast with native speakers: "Your level of Dutch is native and mine is total beginner... It's hard to find the middle ground. I don't understand a lot of what she's saying." (Nina interview)
   - Contrast with language cafes: "In taalcafes everyone comes with different levels. They're not teachers. So you're just mumbling together. You don't even know if what you're learning is correct." (Nina interview)

5. **Finding practice is hard (documented pain):**
   - Community pattern: "I've been trying to find other beginners to practice speaking with, but it's been harder than I expected." (Reddit)
   - "Dutch partner won't speak Dutch" — even native partners switch to English.

### Section 6: How to Know If a Course Will Actually Make You Speak
**Word target:** 300
**Key argument:** Practical criteria for evaluating any course. Genuine advice that applies to competitors too.

**Evidence:**

1. **Evaluation criteria (from knowledge base):**
   - What counts as completing a lesson? (Exercise completion vs. production demonstration)
   - How much speaking practice is included? When does it start?
   - Does practice match your level?
   - Can you practice on your schedule?

2. **Customer interview (why these criteria matter):**
   - On scheduling: "I had to be flexible time-wise but also place-wise. It was preferable for me to do it online." (Nina interview)
   - On classroom limitations: "If you're in a class of 12, the teacher can't give you attention full time. It's impossible. You might get to speak a little bit, but the teacher cannot correct you all the time." (Nina interview)
   - On guided structure: "I like that it's guided. I don't have to think about what now, what do I do now. I don't have the cognitive load of trying to engineer my lesson." (Nina interview)

3. **Match to different goals (from knowledge base):**
   - Some learners need peer pressure → classroom is better
   - Some want a personal teacher relationship → private tutor is better
   - Some just want to pass fast → memorization courses are faster
   - Self-motivated learners who want to retain → production-based courses
