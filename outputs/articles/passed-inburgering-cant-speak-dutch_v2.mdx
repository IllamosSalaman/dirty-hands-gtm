# Why You Pass Inburgering But Can't Speak Dutch (And How to Fix It)

You passed the inburgering exam. You studied for months. You recognized all the correct answers. But when your Dutch neighbor asks "Hoe gaat het?", you freeze. Most inburgering courses train recognition (picking correct answers) rather than production (creating sentences yourself). The exam tests whether you can identify Dutch patterns, not whether you can produce them in conversation.

This experience is common among expats preparing for the A2 inburgering exam.

A common frustration in Dutch learner communities: "I understand everything, but when it's time to talk, I freeze." The "pass but can't speak" complaint appears across expat forums and language learning communities. The pattern is well documented.

Language learning platform Taalhammer describes the same problem: "If you've ever tried learning Dutch, you've probably noticed how quickly isolated words fade away. You can memorize fiets, huis, and eten all week, and still stumble when trying to use them in conversation."

The gap between passing the exam and speaking Dutch is documented, predictable, and fixable. Understanding why it happens is the first step.

## Why You Can Understand Dutch But Can't Speak It

The gap between understanding and speaking is the difference between recognizing language when you see or hear it and creating language yourself. Recognition is easier and develops faster. If you moved to the Netherlands for work or to join your partner, you may understand Dutch conversations but struggle to participate in them. Your passive vocabulary (what you recognize) is always larger than your active vocabulary (what you can produce).

Seidlitz Education, an education publisher focused on language development, explains the problem: "Comprehension is not enough if the students are to demonstrate their knowledge through speaking or writing. Words need to travel from the passive lexicon into the active lexicon so we can retrieve them on-demand. This transfer does not happen on its own."

This gap exists because recognition and production work differently. When you take a multiple choice test, you see the correct answer among the options. Your brain only needs to recognize which one matches what you learned. When you speak, your brain must retrieve the word from memory, construct the sentence, conjugate the verb, and produce the sounds. These are different skills that require different practice.

Here is the key insight: production practice builds recognition, but recognition practice does not build production. When you retrieve a word from memory and use it in a sentence, you strengthen both skills. You become better at producing the word and better at recognizing it. When you practice recognizing a word in a multiple choice exercise, you only strengthen recognition. The harder work of production creates stronger memories than the minimal effort of recognition. This is why courses that train production prepare you for the entire exam, including the reading and listening sections, while courses that train only recognition leave you unprepared for speaking and writing.

Most inburgering courses measure progress by exercises completed. You click through screens. You match words to pictures. You fill in blanks. You pick the correct option from four choices. Every exercise trains recognition. None of them train production.

The exam format reinforces this. Multiple choice questions reward recognition. Fill-in-the-blank exercises reward recognition. Even the listening comprehension section rewards recognition (you identify what was said, not create a response). The speaking and writing exams require production, but by then you've spent weeks or months training the wrong skill.

The result: you pass because you can recognize Dutch. You cannot speak because you never practiced producing it.

## Why Most Inburgering Courses Work This Way

Most inburgering courses focus on recognition exercises because they are easier to build and measure. Exercises with clear right answers can be auto-graded. Progress bars fill up as you complete modules. Learners feel they are making progress.

Building a course that requires you to actually produce language is harder. You need a system that can evaluate conversations. You need to define what counts as successful production. You need to track whether learners actually use words in context, not just see them in exercises.

Progress measurement also favors exercise-based courses. "80% of exercises completed" is easy to track. "Can form sentences using the vocabulary" requires testing actual production, which is more complex.

These courses do help people pass the exam. One course review says: "Within 45 days I passed all exams." Memorization works for passing. The problem is what happens after. You have a certificate that says you know Dutch, but you still cannot order coffee in Dutch.

## Why the Speaking Exam Preparation Isn't Enough

The inburgering speaking exam (spreekexamen) is the hardest component for most test-takers because it requires production under pressure with no chance to edit your response. DutchReview explains: "The spreken examen is often named as one of the hardest exams for internationals because, unlike with reading, listening, or writing, there's almost no chance to edit your work."

This difficulty reveals the problem. If courses trained production throughout, the speaking exam would feel natural. Instead, courses treat speaking as one more section to cram for at the end.

Most courses follow a predictable pattern: reading module, listening module, writing module, speaking module. The speaking module comes last, after weeks or months of recognition-based training. By then, learners have built strong recognition skills and weak production skills. They memorize example responses for the speaking exam the same way they memorized vocabulary.

This works well enough to pass. You memorize enough phrases to survive the timed responses. But memorized phrases do not transfer to real conversation. When your Dutch colleague asks something unexpected, the memorized responses do not help.

The alternative: speaking practice in every lesson from the start. Production woven throughout the course, not added at the end.

## How to Actually Learn to Speak Dutch, Not Just Pass the Test

**How to build real Dutch speaking ability**: Choose a course where lessons complete when you can use the vocabulary, not when you finish exercises. Practice speaking daily, not just before the exam.

The difference is what counts as "done." In most courses, a lesson is done when you click through all the screens. In a course focused on production, a lesson is done when you have actually used the words in conversation.

This works because producing language requires more effort than recognizing it. When you recall a word from memory to use in conversation, that effort strengthens the memory. When you recognize a word in an exercise, the effort is minimal. The harder work of production is what makes learning stick.

Finding speaking practice is surprisingly difficult for expats in the Netherlands. Many learners report that finding practice partners is harder than expected.

Classroom courses have limited speaking time per student. With 15 students and one teacher, individual practice time is constrained. Private tutors charge per hour. Dutch people often switch to English when they hear you struggling. Language exchange partners require scheduling and may not match your level.

One advantage of practicing with a structured course: the speaking practice matches your current level. You are not trying to keep up with a native speaker or explain to a beginner what you need. Every conversation happens at the difficulty you are learning.

## How to Evaluate Any Dutch Course

**Checklist for evaluating Dutch courses**: Ask these questions before choosing a course. The answers reveal whether the course will help you speak Dutch or just pass the exam.

### 1. When is a lesson considered complete?

Some courses complete when you click through all screens. Others complete when you demonstrate you can use what you learned. Ask the course provider: "What do I need to do to complete a lesson?" The answer tells you what the course values.

### 2. How much speaking practice is included?

Some courses offer no speaking practice. Others offer limited sessions. Others offer practice whenever you want. More speaking practice builds more speaking ability. Ask how often you will actually speak Dutch during the course.

### 3. When does speaking practice start?

Courses that add speaking at the end treat it as one more thing to cram for. Courses that include speaking throughout build production skills from day one. Ask: "When does speaking practice start in your course?"

### 4. Does the practice match your level?

Speaking with native speakers can be intimidating when you are a beginner. Speaking with other beginners means neither of you can correct mistakes. Ask whether the course provides practice at your current level.

### 5. Can you practice on your own schedule?

Scheduled sessions with a teacher are valuable but require coordination. Practice you can do anytime fits around work and family. Consider which matters more for your situation.

Different goals need different tools. If you need peer pressure to stay committed, classroom courses provide that. If you want a personal relationship with a teacher, private tutors offer it. If you need to pass quickly and retention is not a priority, memorization courses are faster.

For self-motivated learners who want to pass the exam and actually use Dutch afterward, look for courses where production is the measure of progress, not exercise completion. If you are still evaluating whether a course is worth the investment, ask these questions before deciding.
