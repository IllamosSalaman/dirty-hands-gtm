# What Production-Based Language Learning Is (and Why Dutch Finally Sticks)

You can read a fair amount of Dutch, you finished the course or the app, and you still freeze when you have to say something out loud. Most of what you studied has faded. The cause is structural: almost every Dutch course counts a lesson as finished when you have recognized the material, not when you have produced it.

## What production-based language learning is

Production-based language learning is an approach where you learn vocabulary and grammar by producing the language yourself, speaking and writing it, instead of only recognizing it in exercises. A lesson is finished when you can use the material in a real conversation, not when you have completed the drills. Because you practice producing the language and not just recognizing it, it sticks: you keep what you learn and can use all four skills, reading, listening, speaking, and writing, rather than memorizing for a test and forgetting it afterward.

Production-based language learning is not the same as project-based language learning, sometimes shortened to PBLL. Project-based learning organizes study around completing a project, such as making a video or planning a trip. Production-based learning is about the standard you hold each lesson to. It counts as done when you have produced the language, not when you have seen it.

The principle is easy to state and easy to skip. Most study time goes into taking Dutch in: reading it, listening to it, choosing the right answer. Production-based learning shifts the work to putting Dutch out, saying it and writing it yourself, from memory, before the lesson is allowed to count. The shift is small on paper and large in result, because the half you produce is the half you remember.

## Recognition and production are two different skills (and only one of them sticks)

**Recognition and production are two different skills, and only the one you practice produces lasting Dutch.** Recognition is the receptive half of the language: reading and listening, where Dutch comes in. Production is the productive half: speaking and writing, where you build Dutch yourself. Getting good at one does not make you good at the other.

Take a single word, appel. You can recognize it instantly on a flashcard, in a subtitle, in your neighbor's sentence. That is recognition, and it grows quickly because the word is sitting right in front of you. Producing it is a different task: building a sentence with appel yourself, out loud, when nothing on the screen is prompting you. People who can follow plenty of Dutch often discover the gap the moment they try to speak.

One learner who could understand a lot of Dutch put it plainly: "My vocabulary is okayish, I understand a lot, but speaking and pronunciation is... no. This is something my brain just cannot work around." The vocabulary was there for recognition. It was not there for production, because it had never been built that way.

This split has a name in language research. Merrill Swain's output hypothesis, set out in 1985, argued that understanding a language is not enough on its own to produce it. Taking the language in lets you grasp meaning. Producing it forces you to assemble word order and grammar yourself, which is a separate mental step. Swain drew the idea from immersion students who had years of input and still could not produce correct sentences.

Producing the language also changes how well you hold onto it. In a 2018 study in the journal Psychological Science, Hopman and MacDonald found that learners who practiced producing a new language understood it better afterward than learners who only practiced understanding it. Building the language yourself, rather than only taking it in, is what moves it into memory you can reach later.

So the skills you never produce are the first to fade. You can pour months into reading and listening and still be left with [a language you recognize but cannot reach when you open your mouth](/blog/why-cant-speak-dutch-after-studying).

## Why you forget what you "learned"

**You forget what you "learned" because finishing exercises mostly trains recognition, and recognition fades fast once the course ends.** Re-reading a chapter and clicking the right answer feel like progress because they are easy. That ease is the problem. Practice that asks almost nothing of you leaves almost nothing behind.

This is the comfort trap. Recognition is smooth and pleasant, so it feels like the most productive way to study, and that smoothness is exactly why so little of it stays. The harder work of producing the language is the work that builds memory, and it is the work most courses leave out.

A learner who had just finished an A1 course described the result: "I did it, I finished it. But I didn't feel like I retained anything." She had done everything the course asked. The course asked for recognition, so recognition is what it gave back, and recognition faded.

Part of what makes this hard to see is how low the bar has quietly dropped. For many expats preparing for the A2 or B1 inburgering exam, forgetting has become the expected outcome, which is why so many people pass the inburgering exam and still can't speak Dutch. As one learner observed, "People don't even expect that at A2 you can have a conversation." When that becomes normal, you start to look for the fault in yourself.

So the story shifts from the method to your own ability. The method only ever trained the half of Dutch that fades, and that is a design choice you can work around. Change what the practice asks of you and the result changes with it.

## The clearest symptom: apps that teach phrases you will never say

**The clearest symptom of recognition-only study is an app that teaches phrases you will never say.** Recognition-based tools reward streaks and correct taps, so you can keep a long streak and still end up able to produce only a handful of stock sentences you would rarely use.

Two people who had never met described the same outcome in almost the same words. One said, "I can only say: I have a green apple." Another, recalling a popular app, said, "I learned how to say phrases like: my elephant is green. Where am I gonna use this?" Strangers landing on nearly identical absurd sentences is the signature of practice that drills recognition and never asks you to build something real.

Duolingo is the example most people know. It is genuinely good at building a daily habit and lowering the barrier to starting, and that has real value. It is built around recognizing and selecting answers, though, so the sentences you can produce on your own at the end tend to be the ones it fed you. The format trains the receptive half well and leaves the productive half mostly untouched.

## What production-based learning looks like in practice

**In practice, production-based learning means a lesson is done when you have used the words in conversation, not when you have finished the exercises.** You learn each word by producing it: saying it and writing it in several different sentences, from memory, until you can use it without prompts.

Concretely, that means practicing each new piece of Dutch across all four skills rather than just reading it. You read a sentence and say it, you hear it and say it, then you write it, all before moving on. Reading and listening keep building, and speaking and writing get trained directly, which is the half most study skips.

This is how we built DutchwithJoost. A lesson there has two phases. First you practice each sentence by reading and saying it, hearing and saying it, and writing it. Then you have a conversation with Joost, who stays in the lesson's situation, ordering at a pharmacy or meeting a neighbor, and prompts you to use the words yourself. DutchwithJoost considers a lesson complete when you have used most of the new vocabulary in conversation.

The benefit is retention you can feel. Because you produced the words, they are there when you need them. As one learner put it, "The words that you learn you use... it helps me communicate." Another said what kept them going was the absence of rote drilling: "There is no memorizing, which I really like, because I hated spending two hours on one rule memorizing a million words that don't make sense together."

Joost also stays at your level. One learner described it simply: "Joost knows your level. He's not gonna use C2 level Dutch to reply to you." That matters for production, because you can only practice building sentences if the replies stay close to where you are.

None of this is a rule that only one course can follow. Production-based learning is a principle, and you can apply it with a tutor, a workbook, or a free app, as long as you are the one producing the Dutch. What matters is the standard for a finished lesson, not the tool you use. ## How to add production to any study routine

**You can add production to any study routine, with whatever tools you already use, by making yourself produce the Dutch instead of only reviewing it.** Four habits do most of the work, and they cost nothing to start.

1. **Retrieve, do not re-read.** Cover the Dutch and produce it from the meaning. Instead of reading a list of word pairs, look at the English or the picture and say and write the Dutch from memory. If it does not come, check, then try it again later the same day.
2. **Narrate your day aloud.** Describe what you are doing in Dutch as you do it: making coffee, walking to the station, planning your afternoon. It turns dead time into production practice and shows you exactly which everyday words you cannot yet reach.
3. **Write before you check.** Write a few sentences of your own before you look at the model answer. The effort of producing first, even when you get it wrong, is what builds the memory. Checking afterward then means something.
4. **Find level-matched conversation.** Seek people or tools that will speak close to your level, so you spend the time producing Dutch rather than getting lost.

That last habit matters more than it sounds. Practicing with someone whose Dutch sits far above yours often stalls, because their replies stay out of reach and you spend the conversation lost instead of producing. Matched practice keeps you where you can actually build sentences. None of these habits depends on a particular course. Pair them with an app, a class, or a tutor and each one gets more out of the time you already spend. Several of them work even if you are practicing Dutch speaking on your own.

## Production-based learning vs other common approaches

**Production-based learning differs from the approaches most people have already tried on one point: whether the method ever forces you to produce the language.** Grammar classes, app drilling, and practicing with a native partner each build real recognition. Each can leave production, and retention, untouched.

Traditional grammar classes give you rules and structure, which some learners want. They can also involve little speaking. One learner described an A1 course as "very boring grammar rules... no speaking at all," and finished it without feeling she had retained much. The rules went in through recognition. Producing Dutch was never required.

App drilling builds a habit and a base of recognized words, as covered above. Left on its own, it tends to give back the sentences you were fed, because tapping the right answer is not the same as building one.

Practicing with a Dutch partner or friend sounds ideal, and it can help, but the level mismatch is real. One learner explained why it stalled with her partner: "Your level of Dutch is native and mine is total beginner... It's just impossible to learn." Native speakers also often find it hard to explain the language they use. As she put it, they "can't really explain why it is like that. They just do it."

Each of these has a real place. The gap they share is production. Add deliberate production to any of them and they get stronger.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the difference between recognition and production in language learning?

Recognition is understanding a language when you see or hear it: reading and listening, the receptive half. Production is building the language yourself: speaking and writing, the productive half. They are separate skills, and practicing one does not automatically train the other, which is why you can understand far more Dutch than you can say. Production is also the half that makes the language stick, because using a word yourself is what moves it into memory you can actually retrieve.

### Why do I forget Dutch after finishing a course?

Most courses count a lesson finished when you have clicked through the exercises, which mainly trains recognition, and recognition fades fastest once you stop. Re-reading and multiple-choice feel like progress because they are easy, but that ease is exactly why little of it stays. You keep the Dutch you have produced yourself, so if a course never made you speak or write the words, there is little for your memory to hold onto.

### Why can I understand Dutch but not speak it?

Because understanding and speaking are two different skills. Reading and listening let Dutch come in, and you can get good at those while still being unable to build a sentence on the spot. Speaking develops only when you practice producing the language yourself, out loud, in real exchanges. If your study has been mostly input, the gap you feel is expected, not a sign you are bad at languages.

### Does production-based learning help you remember Dutch longer?

Yes. When you learn a word by using it, speaking and writing it rather than only recognizing it, you build the retrieval pathway that memorization skips, so the word is there when you need it. Producing the language also improves how well you later understand it, so the benefit runs both ways. The practical payoff is retention: you keep what you learn instead of memorizing it for a test and losing it.

### Is production-based learning the same as project-based language learning?

No. Project-based language learning (PBLL) organizes study around completing a project, such as making a video or planning a trip. Production-based learning is about the completion standard for any lesson: it is finished when you have produced the language, spoken and written it, not just recognized it. The two share a search term but describe different things.
