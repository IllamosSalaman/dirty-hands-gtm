## H1: What Production-Based Language Learning Is (and Why Dutch Finally Sticks)

### Opening definition paragraph (AEO snippet target) — part of S1
- **AEO directive:** First paragraph must answer "what is production-based language learning" completely and stand alone for AI lift. Include the retention payoff and the PBLL disambiguation inside the paragraph.
- **Draft spine (writer may polish, keep it self-contained):** Production-based language learning is an approach where you learn vocabulary and grammar by producing the language yourself, speaking and writing it, rather than by recognizing it in exercises. A lesson is finished when you can use the material in a real conversation, not when you have finished the drills. Because you practice producing the language and not just recognizing it, it sticks: you keep what you learn and can use all four skills, reading, listening, speaking, and writing, instead of memorizing for a test and forgetting. It is different from project-based language learning (PBLL), which organizes study around completing a project. Production-based learning is about the completion standard: that you have produced the language, not just recognized it.

### S2. Recognition and production are two different skills (and only one of them sticks)
- **Word target:** 380
- **Key argument:** Map across four sub-skills. Recognition = receptive half (reading, listening), Dutch goes in. Production = productive half (speaking, writing), you build Dutch yourself. Training the receptive half doesn't transfer to the productive half. Then retention: producing the language is what moves it into durable, retrievable memory, so the skills you never produce are the ones you lose first. Concrete contrast: recognizing "appel" on a flashcard vs constructing a sentence with it unprompted.
- **AEO formatting:** Write the receptive-vs-productive distinction as a tight, standalone quotable sentence. Open the section with the distinction, then evidence.
- **Proof points:**
  - Tier 2 (anonymize to "one learner who could follow a lot of Dutch"): "My vocabulary is okayish, I understand a lot, but speaking and pronunciation is... no. This is something my brain just cannot work around." — the lived shape of the gap.
  - **Academic #1 — Swain (1985), Output Hypothesis** on the claim *recognition and production are different skills / input alone is not enough*: state plainly first, then anchor once (input alone left immersion learners able to understand but not produce). Source: `research-sources` Tier 1.
  - **Academic #2 — Hopman & MacDonald (2018), *Psychological Science*** on the *different* claim *producing the language improves later retention and comprehension*: learners who practiced producing outperformed those who only practiced understanding.
